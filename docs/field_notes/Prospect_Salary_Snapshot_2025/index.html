An snapshot of salary research for prospect dev roles curated from LinkedIn, public databases, and more.

Overview

This is exactly what it sounds like: a little snapshot of industry data, independently curated. I’m not trying to compete with the Apra International Salary Survey. Actually this part 1 post is more about how I’m gathering the data, and is more proof of concept, rather than readable report. I’m trying to document my work as I go along.

The Good News

The data I have gathered is free to explore, though it is missing many useful columns at the moment. I have over 1700 individual salary observations from 2012 to 2026, which is not bad, considering I’m maybe 10% of the way through my identified potential sources.

The Bad News

Big shocker: you probably won’t get rich as a prospect development professional. However, it does seem like there is more stability in the higher education sector, similar to government jobs (at least, historically), compared to other types of analysts within industry. It feels like Business Intelligence analysts are often on the chopping block for layoffs because they are seen as helpful, but non-essential. I see really talented people on LinkedIn with much better resumes than me suddenly needing a new gig. I guess the trick is to get into management, not just analysis, or else continue to be a sacrificial lamb for the sake of an IPO, or whatever. I digress.

If you want stronger methodology, more data across sectors beyond higher education, broader scope, and mild abuse of the “3d” effect for bar charts…you’ll have to wait until the next Apra Salary Survey comes out in 2027.1 I limited myself to public information, which as any prospect researcher will tell you, can sometimes be prone to massive gaps.

The major highlight so far has been seeing all the different job titles, which will help with future research. My current process is not very good at capturing “multiple hat” roles where prospect development is only one part of the job - and I’m thinking I can use some natural language processing to help me with that categorization. Considering I’m relying heavily on “prospect” as a keyword, many of the multiple hat roles - sometimes with vague titles like “development coordinator” - are being missed.

The smaller the shop, the more likely you are to wear multiple hats: database admin, gift processing, etc. Major gift officers also do a plenty of prospect research, independent of support staff.

Salary Sourcing & Pre-processing

Bulk Data

I made a data request to the National Labor Exchange (NLx) Research Hub, to try and get access to a larger corpus of online job adds, occupation codes, tools, salaries, etc. However, I don’t think my research case is as compelling as the guys at Loyola who trained an entire model on billions of data points to make very slick job analysis modules in Python. Luckily, their Job Ad Analysis Toolkit will help me with my unstructured text problem - which has been building up as I grab job descriptions and other data from LinkedIn. However, their giant corpus, as far as I know, is under an agreement with NLx that may specify what they can or can’t publish.

I need the job-level data so I can filter for prospect dev roles, and I’m tempted to email them to ask for a subset of their corpus, but again, the NLx agreement may prevent that.

National bulk data for prospect dev roles remains out of reach, for now, so I had to do a state-by-state approach, starting with large public universities.

Large Public Universities

Some notes below about the data aggregation process for states where I dug deeper than national websites like OpenGovPay.com - which seem to have pretty major gaps. Those gaps were what drove me to state-level websites in the first place. When I ran into years where I knew for certain there should be salary data, because the development department existed and had prospect development professionals on the payroll, but the data didn’t fully filter into whatever source those websites use.

California

Data: University of California compensation

Years: 2017-2024

Note: searched by title, used “Prospect”, there may be other key words to use or job titles, arguably more data in there - especially since digital records go back to 2010, just need to find the right keywords Problems: This is the first time I’m seeing Gross Pay vs. Regular pay vs. overtime pay vs. other pay. I have realized I am actually not 100% sure about whether some of my other data is gross, regular, or some mysterious aggregate. Generally speaking, I’m always taking the lower number, with no extras (e.g. benefits) for this version of the dataset. I’m still saving all the other data, but don’t want to bother with it yet. California has one of the larger fundraising worker populations in the country, and is a great example of why cost of living analyses will be important to compare across states and get more accurate averages. Their higher pay will drive up the mean and make the salaries across the country seem rosier than they actually are.

Ohio

Data: from The Buckeye Institute, and University-specific dashboards

Years: 2012-2025

Note: Added over 50 new salaries. I have data from The Ohio State University, but I’m thrown by the job titles, do they call fundraisers “Prospect Development Specialists” over there? Will have to do some LinkedIn research, for now, I’m keeping all that data out of this sample dataset. It seems like one of the most important filters when dealing with bulk downloads and data dashboards for higher education at the state-level, is the name of the department. Whether it’s “Advancement Services” or “University Development”, or whatever, it’s the fastest way to pull as much of the relevant data quickly. However, those department names change over time - and I wasn’t paying as close attention to that phenomenon earlier in the project, which makes me think I may have relied too much on “Advancement” as a keyword, while neglecting “Development”…

Years: 2026

Note: Only added 5 new salaries, no idea how to get at the historical data for Idaho via their dashboard, if that’s even possible. In this case there was a mix of hourly and salaried, and when they are marked full-time I assume 40 hour weeks. However, I’m only marking salary observations as estimates when they are not tied to a specific individual (e.g. from a job description, or payscale with no names).

Foundation-level data

Some data cannot be found via state portals or national portals, and have to be tracked down by looking at any given university foundation’s published salary tables, expenditures, etc. At least to get estimates. The problem, it turns out, is that Universities are often politically-fraught and budgets can be controversial. A few states, like New Mexico and Vermont, had investigative journalists who looked at the 990s for the big public universities in their state and then published paychecks or commentary - and sometimes led to public blowback. Usually lost in the conversation is the fact that a university foundation is invariably a revenue generating entity, and people make wild assumptions about paychecks that aren’t legally required to be disclosed.

The IRS requires that the top-paid administrators for a foundation have their compensation listed, with the rest being reported in one consolidated number. Most journalists are not assuming that the “hidden” millions of dollars in paychecks include jobs like prospect development - ones that don’t have eye-popping numbers, but add to the overall total. Also left out of the conversation is the damage that staff turnover can have for ongoing fundraising operations.

Anyway, I found those “scandals” fascinating, because they impact this project. In states where there has been public conversations about salaries, foundations may not necessarily be as forthcoming if there are no state-level sunshine laws forcing their hand.

Usually this is just the case with the biggest public universities - the smaller public colleges seem perfectly fine publishing at least salary tables…

Next steps

  • Get all states represented
  • Use the Job Ad Analysis Toolkit to make heirarchies based on job title, or work on developing a heirarchy methodology
  • Adding very basic cost of living estimates at the state (not county) level. Better than nothing.
  • Use a linear regression (or something?) to make estimates for same-title roles in years that are missing. So if I have “Prospect Research Analyst” in Utah for 2012, 2014, 2015, etc… but I’m missing 2013, I’d like to try and fill in that gap. Estimates will always be labeled. This will make for an interesting expanded dataset.
